## [Heading 3] Introduction


One of the main areas of artificial intelligence today is Natural Language Processing (NLP), which seeks to enable robots to read, decode, and write human language. Named Entity Recognition (NER) can be considered in this field. Named Entity Recognition (NER) is a fundamental NLP task that identifies and classifies important entities such as persons, locations, organizations, dates, percentages, monetary values, and miscellaneous categories within text.

Considered one of the easiest jobs involving sequence labeling. Labeling and recognizing words or phrases that refer to specific entities (people, locations, organizations, dates) and correspond to a set of preset classes is the aim of Named Entity Recognition (NER), one of the most fundamental NLP tasks.  Information extraction, question answering, summarization, and sentiment analysis are just a few of the downstream applications that frequently use the NER.

Named Entity Recognition (NER) are the core NLP tasks, which form the basis of such advanced tasks as machine translation, information retrieval, question answering, and text summarization. The Linguistic richness of Bangala, however, with its complicated morphology, free word order and high-level ambiguity in the expression of entities, is an obstacle to creating correct and effective models. Current methods typically use independent models to perform NER, which raises computing costs and contextual insight. The lack of large, annotated corpora in Bengali implies that there is very little to use and adapt when training or refining NLP models of the language. Moreover, it is possible to train different models on similar tasks e.g. training Named.




## [Heading 3] Motivation


The seventh most spoken language in the world, with almost 250 million speakers, Bangla is of immense cultural and linguistic importance. Although widely used, Bangali is a poorly represented language in Natural Language Processing (NLP) studies relative to languages that have a large amount of resources, including English. The creation of computational tools in Bangali is not only critical to language technology development, but also to maintain inclusivity in the global digitalization arena.

In addition to requiring a lot of time and computing power, entity recognition (NER) are ineffective. Nevertheless, the existing methods do not generalize very well mainly because they have inadequate contextual information on Bangla text. This study offers a hybrid model that Named Entity Recognition (NER) into a single Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) system in order to overcome these issues. The strategy will focus on improving the learning of contextualised word representations which will dramatically decrease the needs of NLP jobs in Bangla.

The primary objective of this research is to develop and evaluate an effective Named Entity Recognition (NER) model for Bangla using a BiLSTM-based architecture capable of learning contextual features and improving entity classification accuracy. The performance of the proposed NER system is assessed using widely accepted evaluation metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score to ensure reliable and comprehensive measurement of its effectiveness. Furthermore, the study aims to enhance model generalization and reduce overfitting by incorporating techniques such as regularization, dropout, and optimized training strategies, ultimately improving the robustness and adaptability of the NER model across diverse Bangla text patterns.

## [Heading 3] Methodology


This chapter outlines the methodology used in this study to develop a deep learning–based model for Named Entity Recognition (NER) in Bangla text using a Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) architecture. The chapter is divided into key components, beginning with data preprocessing, which includes cleaning, tokenizing, and encoding Bangla text into a suitable format for model training. It also explains the input representation, where token embeddings are used to capture semantic information necessary for accurate entity recognition.
The methodology further describes the BiLSTM architecture, which is designed to capture contextual information from both preceding and succeeding words in a sequence. A task-specific output layer is then used to predict NER tags for each token. The training process includes the use of an appropriate loss function and optimization strategy to ensure efficient learning and improved performance.
Finally, the chapter presents the evaluation metrics—Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and F1-score—to assess the effectiveness of the proposed NER model. These metrics provide a comprehensive understanding of the model’s ability to correctly identify and classify named entities in Bangla text. The use of BiLSTM enhances the model by analyzing sequences in both forward and backward directions, allowing it to better understand context. This bidirectional capability is particularly important for NER tasks, where the meaning and classification of entities depend heavily on surrounding words. As a result, the proposed approach enables improved contextual learning and contributes to more accurate and reliable Bangla NER performance.

## [Heading 3] Literature Review


Surveying Bangla NLP methods, Sen et. al. included classical methods, machine, and deep learning methods. The absence of big annotated resources, which restrict the generalization of models, was highlighted in the study. They suggested considering multi-task and transfer learning methods, which would allow combining POS tagging and NER models with similar features, which would perform better when resources are limited.

A multilingual transformer embeddings with a CRF layer based on MURIL was designed by Bose and Sarkar into a Bangali and Hindi NER model. Their findings showed that multilingual trained models have a huge positive effect on NER in low-resource languages. They, however, observed that such models, when adapted to the characteristics of Bangla language are still required to deliver the best performance.

Rabbi et. Al. made a machine learning-based annotated Bangali NLP corpus with Python and emphasized that the quality and variety of training data determine the performance of the models. Their article notes that bigger and well annotated corpora are required to support POS tagging and NER which are critical in building effective Bangla NLP systems especially in deep learning models.

Jillur Rahman Saurav et. al. tested the NER and POS tagging of the Bengali language via end-to-end trained BiLSTM-based deep neural network models that were based on CNN and CRF and did not utilize any handcrafted features. They were accurate on POS tagging to 93.86\% but on NER, they were only 0.6285, thus illustrating the relative complexity of NER. They proposed some improvements in terms of co-learning, pretrained embeddings, and transfer learning to achieve better generalization.

Alam et. al. suggested an intermediate system between rule-based and machine learning systems to integrate Bangla NER. It was moderately effective, but not scalable and not domain adaptable, highlighting the problem of rule-based methods. The authors recommended that the work should be focused more on the neural approaches that are data-driven and supported by more annotated corpora to achieve better performance in the future.
A contextually trained, cost-sensitive, model of Bangla NER was suggested by Ashrafi et. al. to address rare and ambiguous named entities. The model was more precise than other models that just concentrated on a challenging case, which proved the usefulness of context-aware learning. Improvements might include adding external sources of knowledge such as gazetteers and domain-specific entities in order to improve performance in more specialized settings.

Jueal Mia et. al. contrasted different techniques of Bangla tagging of POS, ranging between rule-based and contemporary machine learning techniques. Their comparison validated that machine learning models are superior to the traditional methods, but they still have to be further adjusted to the syntactic specifics of Bengali to increase the tagging accuracy and address the language-specific grammars better.

A bidirectional LSTM-CRF model in Bangla POS tagging was proposed by Farsin et. al. . The sequence dependencies were learned by LSTM networks and context-related information was utilized by the CRF layer to improve tagging performance to offer a strong framework of aiding to accurate POS annotation in Bangla.

Sarker presented the open-source package called the "BNLP Toolkit" which offers POS tagging, NER, tokenization and word embeddings. Although the toolkit used pretrained embeddings and was effective in the POS tagging, it had only average results in NER. Notably, it did not utilize multi-task learning or shared representations and it is open to future research to investigate their joint modeling techniques.

A popular option in POS tagging and NER is Huang et. al., creators of the Bidirectional LSTM-CRF sequence tagging model. The LSTM-CRF model helps to strengthen the sequence labeling task by considering sequential dependencies of words and keeping the global structure of the sequence. This architecture has been applied successfully to both Bangla POS tagging and NER as well and has shown itself to be performing well when it comes to working with sequential nature of language data.
Ovi et.al. introduced BANEP, a neural-network based end-to-end POS tagger for Bangla. They developed a model that outperformed classical techniques by harnessing deep learning approaches specifically designed for Bangla’s distinct syntax and morphology. BANEP’s performance reveals the potential of neural network-based methods for language specific NLP tasks, and there is scope for future work in extending this framework to other NER tasks in Bangla, thereby benefiting from joint training of both NER tasks.
Gers et. al. have been key to the development of many subsequent sequence-based NLP tasks such as POS tagging and NER through LSTM recurrent networks. LSTM models are useful to model long-range dependencies in natural language data, and hence are a good fit for Bangla syntactic tasks. Their work has shown that LSTM-based models are successfully able to deal with complex label sequence problems like Named Entity Recognition and POS tagging, paving the way for further advancements in Bangla NLP.

## [Heading 3] Gap Analysis


Limited modeling strategies:
Existing research primarily focuses on comparing classical and deep learning approaches, while advanced, hybrid, or multi-task NER frameworks remain largely unexplored.
Insufficient handling of Bangla linguistic complexity:
Multilingual embedding-based NER models do not adequately capture Bangla’s morphological richness, inflectional variations, and contextual dependencies.
Over-reliance on gazetteer or rule-based methods:
Several NER approaches depend heavily on gazetteers or handcrafted rules, which restrict scalability, robustness, and adaptability to new domains.
Small-scale and domain-specific evaluations:
Most Bangla NER systems are evaluated on small or single-domain datasets, raising concerns about their ability to generalize across larger or multi-domain corpora.
Lack of robust end-to-end NER systems:
Existing end-to-end NER models lack comprehensive validation in real-world or cross-domain environments.
Limited use of Transformer-based NER models:
Although Transformer-based techniques have been explored, Bangla-specific, context-aware, and resource-efficient NER models remain insufficiently developed.


## [Heading 3] Summary


This chapter presents a comprehensive overview of the development of Bangla Natural Language Processing (NLP), with a particular focus on Named Entity Recognition (NER). The literature review highlights the evolution of approaches from rule-based and machine learning methods to more advanced deep learning and transformer-based models. While several studies have contributed significantly by proposing multilingual embeddings, annotated datasets, and deep learning architectures such as BiLSTM and CRF, many limitations still exist.
The findings indicate that Bangla NER systems are often constrained by the lack of large annotated datasets, limited handling of linguistic complexity, and dependence on rule-based or gazetteer-based methods. Additionally, many existing models are evaluated on small or domain-specific datasets, which limits their generalization capability. Although recent advancements include contextual and transformer-based approaches, their application in Bangla remains limited and computationally expensive.
Overall, the gap analysis reveals the need for more robust, scalable, and context-aware NER models that can effectively address Bangla’s linguistic challenges and perform well in low-resource environments. This motivates the development of improved deep learning–based approaches for Bangla NER in the subsequent chapters.

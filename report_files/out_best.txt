## [Heading 1] ABSTRACT

This research explores automatic question generation in Bangla using two state-of-the- art transformer-based language models: multilingualT5 and BanglaT5. As Bangla is a low-resource language in the field of Natural Language Processing, this study aims to bridge the resource gap by developing a system capable of generating grammatically correct and contextually relevant factoid questions from Bangla paragraphs. For the purpose of facilitating this, we have developed a manually constructed dataset of 3,000 paragraph-level question pairs from various sources to ensure topically and linguistically diverse information. We fine-tuned both the models using the same dataset and preprocessing routine using the Hugging Face library for model training and optimization. Automatic as well as manual evaluation was performed. Automatically, the models were graded using the Exact Match and ROUGE-L F1 criteria. The MultilingualT5 model obtained EM of 25.55% and ROUGE-L F1 of 63.56%. On the other hand, BanglaT5 surpassed it to obtain EM of 28.65% and ROUGE-L F1 of 70.77%. This shows the benefit of language-specific pretraining in BanglaT5 to better represent the grammatical and semantic nuances of the Bangla language than the multilingual MultilingualT5. By targeting factoid-style questions, this work makes the notable contribution to Bangla NLP. We have developed a demo application to demonstrate how our model works. The application takes a text input, processes it as required, and generates a question as output to reveal the capabilities of the model. A foundation for further work in question generation and education technology for Bangla is provided by the constructed model and dataset. Overall, the results confirm the value of language-specific model construction and fine-tuning for under-resourced languages and provide a scalable, tangible guideline for constructing QG systems in low-resourced linguistic environments similar to Bangla.

## [Heading 2] Introduction


The dramatic progress in Natural Language Processing has greatly enhanced the functionalities of computers in producing human-like text. Of these functionalities, Question Generation has come out as an important subset, wherein the work is to create grammatically correct, semantically valid, and contextually appropriate questions from an input textual passage. Though much work has been done in rich- resource languages like English, languages like Bangla are far from being well- represented in the absence of resources, corpora, and language-specific models.

Although it is the sixth most widely spoken language in the world, Bangla has not had much success in the task of question generation. The majority of work so far either is in heavy reliance on translated corpora or asks for preset answers, restricting the quality, applicability, and adaptability of the generated questions. Moreover, the syntactic nature of Bangla and its complexity in structure pose specific challenges which are not adequately covered even by universal multilingual models.

To bridge the gap, in this work, an automatic Bangla Question Generation system is presented based on two strong transformer-based models MultilingualT5 and BanglaT5. The system is tailored specifically for generating factoid-type questions, i.e., questions asking specific factual answers from input passages. Through fine- tuning these models on readymade Bangla datasets and fine-tuning them for relevance of generated questions, grammatical correctness, and linguistic variation, the work is designed to provide an accurate solution for directly generating Bangla questions from text without relying on pre-knowledge of answer strings.

Not only does this project meet the requirement of having automated educational resources, but it also makes an important contribution towards developing NLP abilities for low-resource languages such as Bangla. With its emphasis on factoid questions, the system is of great use for applications in educational tests, quizzes, reading comprehension systems, as well as e-learning systems. It thereby lays the

foundation for further advancement of the Bangla language technologies for supporting students, teachers, researchers, as well as the entire community.


## [Heading 2] Motivation

The inspiration for the project is based on the palpable shortage of Bangla Question Generation resources and systems. While English can boast an enormous repertoire of datasets, models, and benchmarks, nothing of that sort for Bangla can be found, despite its enormous speaker count. The majority of the Bangla QG tasks either use plain translations of English or are plagued with inconsistencies in the available data, grammatical distortions, and loss of meaning because of the lack of proper training resources.

Manual question generation for educational tests, quizzes, and reading comprehension tests is time-consuming, labour-intensive, and not always standardized. Automating the generation of questions would bring numerous benefits to various fields such as education, healthcare, business, and language learning systems.

A key driving force for this effort is improving fact-based learning in Bangla. Factoid questions are especially important for tests, since they ensure the ability of learners to remember factual detail correctly. Having a strong Bangla QG system will enable the development of better learning materials by educational institutions as well as make practice for the learners in an interactive, efficient way. Lastly, having successfully deployed a Bangla QG system with recent transformer models such as MultilingualT5 and BanglaT5, the project sets out to introduce scalable solutions for Bangla NLP, relating that even low-resource languages could make use of current AI advancements when presented with careful methodology as well as strategic fine-tuning.

## [Heading 2] Methodology

The system proposed is based on an ordered methodology for ensuring the formulation of well-constructed, contextually appropriate factoid questions in Bangla. The process starts with collecting and preprocessing the data, in which educational sources, online articles, and available public datasets provide the input of Bangla text passages. The texts are processed through tokenization, normalization for compatibility with transformer-based architectures. The subsequent step is the model selection and fine-tuning, in which two strong transformer models are used: MultilingualT5, which is pre trained on the giant multilingual corpus, and BanglaT5, which is fine-tuned for the Bangla language. Both the models are trained on an optimized set of paragraph question pairs complying with the factoid question structure so that they can capture syntactic as well as semantic patterns for question generation.

The outputs of the models are then analyzed through an automated methods. Common NLP measurements like BLEU, ROUGE is utilized in order to gauge similarity as well as relevance between generated questions as well as reference questions Lastly, MultilingualT5 as well as BanglaT5 performance is compared with each other so that their strengths as well as weaknesses in performing the task of the Bangla QG are researched.

## [Heading 2] Literature Review

Extensive research has been conducted in automatic Question Generation for high-

resource languages. Several methodologies have been explored, ranging from rule- based systems and syntactic transformations to advanced deep learning models, especially transformer architectures like T5, Multilingual T5, and GPT variants.

For Bangla, however, QG research is relatively recent and limited. Various studies have sought to develop Bangla QG systems by fine-tuning pre-trained models, translating English datasets, or building bilingual models. A few notable works include:

A. R. Fahad et al. [1] design and develop an answer-agnostic question generation model especially tailored for the Bangla language, hence bridging the critical lack of resources available for Bangla in the area of question generation. For this purpose, the authors utilized the SQuAD_bn dataset, dividing it into 118,000 rows for the training set, 2,500 rows for the validation set, and 2,500 rows for the test set, respectively. In their method, the they fine-tuned three transformer models: Bangla T5, mT5-base, and BanglaGPT2. For optimization of the training phase, two unique data formatting methods were utilized, viz. All Questions Per Line (AQL) and One Question Per Line (OQL), resulting in an emergence of six different model variants. Besides, the investigation analyzed six decoding methods for question generation, viz. commonly utilized methods like beam search and greedy decoding. The authors built two in-house classification models to ensure that generated questions were relevant and grammatically credible. These models registered remarkable scores of 88.54% for relevancy and 95.67% for grammaticality, respectively. Out of the models under investigation, the performance of the mT5 model showed the best performance when trained under the technique of OQL formatting and tested with beam decoding. Especially, it reached a score of 77% for relevancy and 96% for grammaticality. Apart from that, the performance of the model was quantitatively tested with benchmark natural language generation metrics, obtaining scores of 36.60 for BLEU-4, 48.98 for METEOR, and 63.38 for ROUGE-L. Overall This work makes an important addition to the body of research aimed at Bangla natural language processing, providing an effective method for generating high-quality questions independent of pre-existing answers.

J. F. Ruma et al. [2] explore the lack of resources accessible for question generation (QG) in Bengali language through the creation of answer-aware question generation models. They especially fine-tuned transformer architectures of the type T5, with special emphasis on models like BanglaT5. Besides, they reformatted available question-answer resources into creating Bengali-specific QG data in order to allow question generation with high similarity to precise contextual passages and respective answers. For comparative experimentation, the SQuAD_bn dataset was utilized, which contained mostly 132,777 samples. Upon preprocessing steps, the dataset was partitioned into 54,150 samples for training and 13,538 samples for validation. This strict preprocessing guaranteed data relevance and cohesion for the task of question

generation. The performance of three models, BanglaT5, mT5-small, and mT5-base, were evaluated with the application of various decoding algorithms, viz., beam search and greedy decoding. BanglaT5 showed the best performance among these models, especially with BLEU-1 and ROUGE-L evaluation criteria, making it the best model for Bengali QG tasks in this work. Secondly, the questions generated showed an excellent grammatical accuracy with an accuracy percentage of 98%. The principal performance parameters shown are ROUGE-L with the value of 35.77 with beam width of 5, ROUGE-1 value of 37.88 with beam width of 5, ROUGE-2 value of 17.52 with beam width of 5, and BLEU-1 value of 38.63 with beam width of 10. Overall, this work makes an essential contribution towards enhancing the application scope of natural language processing in Bengali language, stressing the significance of fine-tuned transformer models in generating grammatically and contextually accurate questions.

S. Sharma et al. [3] introduce an architecture of bilingual Question-Answering (QA), aimed at generating questions and answers from Bengali and English textual inputs. For each language, the proposed architecture utilizes independent processing modules, which draw upon various natural language processing techniques tailored to the unique linguistic features of both English and Bengali. Syntactic operations like subject-auxiliary inversion are utilized by the system to aid question generation for the English module. However, for Bengali text processing, the model deals with unique features such as bivokti (case markers) and postfixes, which prove essential for grammatical correctness and semantic fidelity in generated questions. The bilingual QA model achieved an overall question generation performance of 78.33%, with an accuracy value of 76% in generating grammatical and contextual questions. Evaluations were done with several benchmarking datasets, demonstrating the robustness and flexibility of the system in various linguistic environments. This work highlights the utility of bilingual systems in education technology and multilingual natural language processing systems. What's more, it highlights the ever-present challenge from the sparsity of Bengali language resources, especially the need for further study and resource construction in the field.

W. Morris et al. [4] present an end-to-end natural language processing pipeline for automated question generation and constructed response grading in the iTELL education framework. It supports state-of-the-art language models, with GPT-3.5 being used for question generation and MPNet and BLEURT models being utilized for grading student responses. The pipeline is evaluated using the MultiRC dataset, which contains 456 source passages and more than 5,000 linked questions. At a performance level, the evaluation models had very good levels of accuracy: 85% using LLaMA-2, 81% using MPNet, and 79% using BLEURT. GPT-3.5 was seen to produce a remarkably good ability to generate questions that are understandable, with a 100% rate of coherence according to human evaluation. Additionally, an inter-rater agreement metric of 0.65 reflected a substantial degree of agreement across different evaluators. Nevertheless, the research identified that GPT-3.5 had some limitations

when required to generate the specific or narrowly framed question types. Unlike most other research within the area, the authors did not specifically report ROUGE or Exact Match scores for their tests. Rather, the effectiveness of the system was supplemented with evidence from user feedback, showing a very good rate of satisfaction: 75.4% of the users reported being satisfied with the system, and 81.7% of them concurred that the tasks introduced by the system led to an increase in their learning outcomes. This research highlights the dramatic doemant of big language models (LLMs) for the composition of scalable and domain-specific tools for pedagogies. It also highlights future opportunities for further development of question generation processes to handle a wider spectrum of question types.

Y. Poon et al.'s [5] research examines the use of Large Language Models (LLMs), specifically Stanford Alpaca based on LLaMA architecture, to generate higher-order reading comprehension questions for use within the PIRLS (Progress in International Reading Literacy Study) education model. The research is directed at designing questions that relate to inferencing, integrating, and evaluation tasks, which are key elements of higher-order reading comprehension. The DuReader_robust dataset was used by the authors for their experiments to evaluate the questions on the basis of usability and conformity with PIRLS categorizations. Importantly, no results were reported using traditional text generation metrics like ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, or ROUGE-L, thus putting a premium on their use within the realm of pedagogic application compared to standard NLP benchmarking. The application of few-shot prompting with LLMs was justified with considerable performance benefits compared to the traditional baseline models, pipeline-based and sequence-to-sequence (seq2seq) based models. Specifically, the LLM-generated questions had 31.5% higher-order questions, much higher than that of pipeline and seq2seq baselines that only yielded 8% and 6.5% higher-order questions, respectively. On questions that did not need post- generation revision the LLM scored a 71% rate of usability. The result highlights the capability of the model to produce pedagogically relevant, fluent, and contextually suitable questions with minimal need for intervention on a human scale. The paper overall highlights the substantial potential for LLMs to transform automated pedagogic technologies, especially within the context of higher-order question generation. It also highlights the lingering issue of the lack of sufficiently diverse, higher-order pedagogic datasets that are also multi-language, calling for further research and dataset production within this area.

M. V. K. RAO et al. [6] provides an Automatic Question and Answer (Q&A) Generation system based on Large Language Models (LLMs) with specific emphasis on applications for educational and legal examinations. The proposed model combines cutting-edge methods with natural language processing (NLP) strategies and multimodal data handling for improving the quality and adaptability of generated content. The proposed model is capable of creating a diversified range of question types, from subjective, objective, to fill-in-the-blank types. The model is designed according

to the cognitive models provided by Bloom’s Taxonomy, ensuring thereby the generation of questions that cover various levels of knowledge and comprehension. The implementation incorporates cutting-edge models like GPT-3, Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq) transformers, and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks for achieving solid performance across a range of domains. Pre-training of the model uses massive datasets, which comprises SQuAD 1.1, SciQ, and S2ORC, covering a diverse range from thousands to a million examples. The performance of the model is tested using hard evaluation measures with a result of ROUGE-1 at 85%, ROUGE-2 at 79%, ROUGE-L at 82%, and Exact Match (EM) at 70%. A key contribution of the research is that pre- training within a specific domain highly improves the relevance and quality of the generated questions, making the system highly flexible across a range of highly specific domains. Despite the success of the proposed method, there are several challenges that are yet to be addressed, which are mainly the improvement of the capabilities of the model to maintain a high contextual relevance and optimizing multimodal data sources integration within question generation. Broadly speaking, the research presents a rising potential for LLM-based methods within automated educational and legal evaluations, while flagging areas for future improvements as well.

## [Heading 4] Similar Applications

Several question generation and question answering systems have been developed for English and multilingual contexts, many of which serve as inspiration for similar advancements in Bangla.

F. Sultana et al. [7] respond to the pressing dearth of comprehensive Question- Answering datasets for the low-resource Bengali language with an innovative Question-Answer Generation pipeline. Their proposed methodology consists of three main modules: an answer span extraction model, question generation model, and roundtrip consistency filtering measure meant to filter away inconsistent or poor- quality QA pairs. To model their pipeline, the authors translated popularly utilized English datasets, namely SQuAD 1.1 and SQuAD 2.0, into Bengali with the help of the NLLB (No Language Left Behind) translation model. Admitting the difficulties involved in direct translation, they proposed an innovative embedding-based answer alignment method meant to enhance the precision of answer translations and maintain the semantic accuracy of the QA pairs.Through this, they were able to produce over 170,000 QA pairs, leading to the establishment of BanglaQA, a large synthetic QA dataset obtained from around 16,000 Bengali news articles classified under five broad themes. This newly created resource signifies an enormous improvement in enabling QA research and applications for the Bengali language. The models trained on BanglaQA showed robust performance, with the best model reaching an F1 metric value of 86.14, which, though slightly under the human baseline of 95.72 F1, reflects an excellent degree of accuracy for a synthetic low-resource QA dataset. Overall, this contribution makes an important contribution to the body of work in Bengali natural language processing insofar as it not only presents an extensible QA generation pipeline but also an accessible dataset which can further be utilized for future research and development efforts.

M. Moron et al. [8] explore the performance of language models and large language models for the tasks of Bangla newspaper article summarization, with special emphasis on abstractive summarization and headline generation. Acknowledging the unavailability of resources for Bangla-language summarization, the authors compiled an entirely new and diverse dataset by scraping articles from ten Bangla online newspapers with both topical diversity and recency. In their experiment, the authors fine-tuned two models: BanglaT5 and Small BanglaT5. Their findings indicated that the models delivered higher performance compared to various general-purpose LLMs. BanglaT5, for instance, registered ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L scores of 25.90, 4.10, and 23.83, respectively. These scores were significantly higher than those registered by GPT-3.5, which yielded ROUGE-1: 20.1, ROUGE-2: 2.12, and ROUGE-L:
17.32. Even models like mT5-multilingualXLSum and mT5-m2m-crossSum lagged behind corresponding performance delivered by the Bangla-specific models. Although GPT-3.5 registered tolerable performance under zero-shot setups, leveraging the

application of Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) fine-tuning to models like Llama-2, TinyLlama, and Gemma did not match corresponding performance delivery by the Bangla-optimized methods. These results highlight the need for language-specific fine- tuning methodologies to improve summarization performance on underrepresented languages like Bangla. The research recommends that for Bangla text summarization endeavors, BanglaT5 is the best choice from which to pursue optimal performance, delivering performance that is better across all performance measures evaluated from general-purpose LLMs. This underscores the need to develop and optimize models tailored specifically for low-resource languages to deliver maximum performance on natural language processing applications.


## [Heading 4] Related Research


E. R. Rhythm et al. [9] investigates the use of deep learning models for Bengali Question-Answering (QA) tasks with a specific focus on mitigating the problem caused by the lack of benchmark datasets and pretrained language models for Bengali. To enable complete evaluation, the scholars translated SQuAD 2.0 into Bengali and further prepared a human-annotated Bengali QA dataset. A zero-shot method based on DistilBERT produced an F1 value of 51.18 and an Exact Match (EM) value of 50.05 during their experimental analyses. Nevertheless, models that were fine-tuned with Bengali-specific data outperformed the zero-shot methods on a consistent basis, showcasing the necessity for domain-specific model adaptations for low-resource languages. The human-annotated evaluation setup gave valuable insights into the current performance difference between machine outputs and human-grade QA generation, highlighting the necessity for further improvements to bridge the performance difference gap. Major contributions of the research include a comparative analysis of a range of Bengali QA models specifically BERT, DistilBERT, and RoBERTa the development of a human-annotated Bengali QA dataset for future benchmarks, and the investigation of the application of transfer learning methods to further improve Bengali natural language processing (NLP) capabilities. Overall, the study contributes to the knowledge of QA system development for Bengali and calls for the benefits brought about by both good datasets and by the application of transfer learning methods for overcoming the intrinsic problem associated with low-resource language NLP tasks.

M. S. Shahriar et al. [10] presents a text-based Question-Answering (QA) system capable of generating and answering questions in various fields. The foremost goal of the proposed system is to aid students, researchers, and instructors by automating part of the learning process, hence making the knowledge acquisition process more efficient. The proposed system exploits deep learning methods and natural language processing models to produce informative and contextual questions and answers. In their experiment, the researchers evaluated the performance of several models, such as BengaliT5 and mT5, in carrying out question-answering tasks. The research made use of the BengaliRQA dataset, which contains 3,000 passages and 14,889 question-answer

pairs. The dataset includes a rich variety of question types, such as factoid, causal, confirmation, and list questions, ensuring the complete assessment of various types of inquiry. Evaluation was carried out using commonly known evaluation metrics such as Exact Match (EM) and F1 values. BengaliT5 showed the best performance with an EM value of 62.42 and an F1 score of 78.11, outscoring other models under evaluation. Overall, this work promotes the development of Bengali-language QA systems and highlights the efficacy of fine-tuned transformer models in producing high-quality educational materials. It also emphasizes the need for domain-specific data and evaluation methods in propelling QA system development in low-resource languages.

## [Heading 2] Summary

In short, although meaningful headway has been made on automated Question Generation for major languages and widely spoken languages, research on Bangla has seen limited and disconnected attempts. Most studies that have so far been carried out either are based on English translated datasets thus compromising linguistic and cultural integrity or cover mostly answer-aware contexts that narrow the generation mechanism by demanding pre-defined answers. These attempts fail to explore the potential to its full extent on answer-agnostic question generation, particularly from source Bangla texts where syntactic and contextual subtleties matter most.

## [Heading 2] Detailed Methodology and Design

The construction of the Bangla Question Generation system required careful planning, experimentation, and tuning over several stages. The methodology was created so that high-quality questions can be generated from Bangla text with both the multilingual MT5 as well as the monolingual BanglaT5 models.

Each phase of the methodology was rigorously crafted according to best practice in Natural Language Processing (NLP) research, fine-tuning Transformer models, and handling multilingual texts.

Data Collection and Preparation
For this study, we created a Bangla dataset to enable the question generation task. The dataset had 3,000 rows with each of them carrying a "Paragraph" and the respective "Question." These paragraphs were sourced from an extremely diverse range of Bangla sources, such as school-level textbooks, Wikipedia pages, and Bangla news portals. This diversity was aimed at ensuring that the dataset covers all possible ranges of Bangla writing styles, vocabularies, and syntactic structures. Each of the paragraphs was thoroughly checked, and respective questions were manually built to stay contextually relevant and factually accurate with the source paragraph. This manual building ensured that the dataset was both semantically and syntactically accurate,

which makes it acceptable for training and testing the performance of transformer- based language models for Bangla question generation tasks.

Preprocessing
Preprocessing is essential in transforming raw data into data understandable to deep learning models. Every data point involved a paragraph-question pair, presented as input-output for a sequence generation task. Preprocessing started with text normalization, where punctuation marks and special characters were normalized, additional whitespaces were removed, and Unicode normalization was performed to deal with the variation in the use of encodings. A task-specific instruction "generate question:" alone was prepended to each input paragraph, which acts as an instruction to the model to effectively learn the task of question generation.

We implemented a custom dataset class Seq2SeqDataset for tokenizing and encoding data with model-specific tokenizers. For mT5, we utilized the SentencePiece tokenizer from the google/mt5-small checkpoint, whereas for BanglaT5, we utilized a Bangla- optimized tokenizer from csebuetnlp/banglat5_qg. Both the "Paragraph" field and the "Question" field were tokenized to 500 input tokens and 100 tokens, respectively, to

align with their natural lengths. Padding and truncation were performed to align input sizes for batch processing.

A custom data collator was developed to handle the conversion of tokenized data into PyTorch tensors, such as attention mask and decoder input. This facilitated batching in training efficiently. The whole dataset was divided into training, validation, and testing sets with respective proportions of 72.25%, 12.75%, and 15%. The dataset features well-balanced input and output lengths, which make it effective for training models for Bangla question generation.

Model Fine-tuning
The setup for fine-tuning mT5 was slightly different. It is a Bangla-based multilingual model trained using Bangla dataset train with the similar configurations of 10 epochs, 1e-3 learning rate, 8 batch size, and with the same 3,000-pair Bangla dataset.


Figure 3.9: Architecture of T5 model

The BanglaT5, a pre-trained encoder-decoder model, fine-tuned on Bangla for 10 epochs, had a learning rate of 1e-3 and a batch size of 8. As this is a sequence-to- sequence task and Kaggle environments do have some resource constraints, the gradient accumulation steps were adjusted to 5. This process enabled the model to mimic larger updates without the memory constraint of training on plus-sized batches. A learning rate scheduler (cosine_with_restarts) is also useful to achieve convergence without sudden drop in the performance of the model. In addition, weight decay was used with a coefficient of 0.01 in order to regularize the model and avoid overfitting, which would occur with long training cycles.

Fine-tuning of mT5 was done using a slightly different setup. It is a multilingual model trained on 101 languages including Bangla, and trained for 10 epochs on the same 3,000-pair Bangla dataset with a learning rate of 1e-3 and batch size of 8. Both BanglaT5 and mT5 were set to evaluate their performance at the end of each epoch, automatically save the best-performing model checkpoint, and log training metrics in detail. Dynamic padding was managed through a custom data collator that also handled attention masks and decoder inputs essential for sequence-to-sequence tasks. Training for both models was conducted using a P100 GPU. By embedding task-specific prompts like “generate question” into the inputs and correctly aligning the decoder input sequences, both models were successfully adapted to perform Bangla question generation with high effectiveness.

These metrics were aimed at measuring both the syntactic correctness and semantic relevance to regard with respect to the reference questions. BLEU was used to measure how many n-grams in the model-generated questions overlapped with the reference questions, giving a fine-grained indication of surface accuracy. We also reported the ROUGE score, especially the ROUGE-L. ROUGE-L, however, is quite effective due to its ability to capture the longest common subsequence (LCS) that an output string has with respect to the input string, which does well in measuring deeper semantic similarity and contextual alignment also.

These measures include standardized scoring methods that have found wide acceptance in the NLP research community to allow objective model comparisons. With the same, the evaluation showed BanglaT5 always beat mT5 in terms of fluency, as well as relevance in context. ## [Heading 2] Performance Evaluation and Comparative Analysis

Chapter  6
Conclusion
This chapter gives an overall conclusion to the Bangla Question Generation project. It gives the summary of the accomplishment, reviews the limitations faced during development, and gives ideas about the directions to further improve the system. The development of the project was aimed to utilize cutting-edge Natural Language Processing (NLP) methods to the Bangla language a relatively low- resource language in the context of AI.

## [Heading 2] Summary


This paper proposes an all-encompassing methodology for generating questions in Bangla using transformer models, namely MT5 and BanglaT5. The main objective was to create an automated, scalable system to produce factoid-type questions in Bangla from input text. We initiated with contextualizing the current gaps in Bangla NLP and question generation studies, mainly the dearth of high-quality native datasets and sparse language-specific models. A thorough literature survey identified seminal studies, bringing to light shortcomings in dataset quality, model flexibility, and evaluation consistency for Bangla QG models.

The research methodology followed a structured process incorporating data collection, preprocessing, model fine-tuning, and evaluation. A manually annotated dataset of 3,000 Bangla paragraph-question pairs was prepared to ensure linguistic and context richness. Preprocessing involved text normalization, tokenization, and dataset division for efficient training. Preprocessing was performed for both the MT5 and BanglaT5 models using Hugging Face’s Trainer API, using the same training parameters to provide a balanced comparison. Automatic metrics like BLEU, ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, ROUGE-L, and Exact Match (EM) were evaluated, supported by human evaluation by native Bangla language speakers.

The findings indicated all-around better performance by BanglaT5, with 28.65% EM and 51.40% BLEU, as opposed to 25.55% EM and 43.49% BLEU for MT5. BanglaT5 performed better even in ROUGE scores, which reaffirmed its better performance due to language-specific pretraining. The best and most precise models, following careful assessment, were incorporated into a Flask web application with meticulous care.

This work is meaningfully contributing to Bangla NLP by demonstrating how structured approaches and transformer architectures are beneficial in overcoming low- resource language processing challenges. In general, the system is a preliminary step toward multilingual, domain-customizable, and more sophisticated Bangla QG tools

with great potential to integrate into educational and scholarly technologies.


## [Heading 2] Limitation


While the project achieved its primary goals, some limitations were encountered which should be noted for transparency and to inform improvements in the future. The most significant constraint was the small size of the dataset. Even though very carefully curated, the dataset was relatively small relative to large-size NLP datasets and impacted the generalizability of the models to widely differing scenarios. Even with explicit attempts at making the data diverse, some bias was manifested in questions generated from unusual or rare topics. In addition, due to the constraints of available hardware only smaller configurations of the MT5 and BanglaT5 models were fine- tunable. More expressive and larger ones similar to MT5-Small or MT5-Large could not be considered due to runtime and memory limitations. In addition, even as the models tended to work well with Bangla grammatical constructs, some outputs grappled with structured sentences with deeply nested clauses and produced slightly lower coherence questions in isolated instances.


## [Heading 2] Future Work

Based on the success of the existing system and its limitations, there are various directions for future work proposed to expand its capabilities further. A higher priority is still to increase the dataset, and larger and diversified Bangla datasets with domain- specific text from science, history, and literature will enable model generalization. With access to higher computational power, future directions might include fine-tuning larger models, or with cutting-edge architectures.

Functionality-wise, the system will be expandable to produce various question types other than factoid, such as descriptive, yes/no, and inference questions to better facilitate education assessment. Deployment will take center stage going forward, with intentions to have a user-friendly interface (UI) and backend services for mobile applications. This will entail the development and implementation of an easy-to-use UI accommodating batch input, dataset upload, real-time generation, and user feedback acquisition.

For compatibility with cell phones, model compression methods like knowledge distillation will be utilized to have efficient execution on resource-restricted platforms like cell phones and tablets. Post-processing modules will also be included to correct minor grammatical mistakes automatically to improve quality of output.

Lastly, opening the system under a liberal license will encourage collaboration from

developers, educators, and researchers, stimulating further development and broader adoption. Future improvements will build the project into a strong, scalable, and accessible tool to significantly impact Bangla language learning and digital empowerment.

## [Heading 2] ABSTRACT


Prepify is a web-based platform built for university students who want to share, organize, and discover handwritten academic notes. In Bangladesh and similar regions, students commonly exchange study materials through informal messaging groups on WhatsApp, Telegram, or Facebook. These notes get buried under newer messages, are hard to search, and vary wildly in quality. Prepify addresses this by providing a structured space where handwritten solutions can be uploaded as photographs, categorized by department and course, and ranked through community voting. The platform is built on Next.js 16.1 and React 19.2 for the front end, PostgreSQL with Drizzle ORM for data storage, and Cloudinary for image hosting. Better Auth with Google OAuth is used for authentication. A points-based leaderboard encourages quality contributions, and a moderation workflow with admin and moderator dashboards keeps content standards high. The platform also uses OCR technology to pull text from handwritten images and large language model APIs to turn rough notes into well-organized study material. Development followed an Agile methodology with iterative prototyping over two semesters. Phase 1 delivered the core platform with authentication, note upload, voting, commenting, follow notifications, and moderation. Phase-2 focuses on the AI pipeline for content extraction and enhancement. Testing confirms reliable handling of multi-image uploads, real-time vote updates, and content moderation workflows.

## [Heading 3] Introduction


University students in Bangladesh rely heavily on handwritten notes for exam preparation. These notes, written during lectures or while solving past exam questions, are typically shared through WhatsApp groups, Facebook Messenger, or Telegram channels. While this informal sharing works in small circles, it breaks down at scale. Notes get buried under newer messages, there is no way to search for specific topics, and quality varies widely with no mechanism to filter helpful content from poor material. The problem compounds over semesters. A student looking for handwritten solutions to a specific course from a previous batch has no reliable way to find them. Even when such notes exist, they sit in someone's phone gallery or in a chat group the student may never have joined. Prepify is a web-based platform designed to solve this problem. It gives students a dedicated space to upload photographs of their handwritten notes, organized by university, department, and course. Other students can browse, vote on, and comment on these notes. A leaderboard tracks the most helpful contributors, encouraging students to share quality work. What sets Prepify apart from general note-sharing platforms is its AI-powered feature pipeline. The platform uses OCR technology to extract text from handwritten images and then sends that extracted text to a large language model API, which expands and restructures the content into well-organized study material. A student uploads a photo of their rough lecture notes, and the system produces a clean, detailed version of the same content.


## [Heading 3] Motivation


The idea for Prepify grew out of a recurring frustration. During exam seasons, students scramble to collect notes from seniors and classmates. They post requests in WhatsApp groups, scroll through hundreds of messages looking for a particular solution, and often end up with blurry photos or incomplete material. The process wastes time and produces inconsistent results. Several factors made this problem worth solving computationally. First, smartphone cameras have improved to the point where handwritten notes can be captured clearly. Second, OCR technology has matured enough that extracting text from handwriting, while not perfect, is practical for many use cases. Third, large language models can take rough, abbreviated notes and produce structured explanations from them. These three capabilities combined make it possible to build something that did not exist before: a platform that accepts a photo of handwritten notes and produces organized study material from it. No existing platform specifically targets handwritten academic notes with AI-powered content enhancement. Platforms like Docsity and Thinkswap focus on typed or general study content behind paywalls. Ocenot in Bangladesh offers a marketplace for notes but without community ranking or AI features. None of them take a photograph of a student’s handwritten work and turn it into something more useful.


## [Heading 3] Objectives


The objectives of this project are as follows:
i. Develop a collaborative web platform for uploading, organizing, and sharing handwritten academic notes.
ii. Implement community engagement features such as voting, ranking, and a contributor leaderboard.
iii. Prepare an AI integration framework for OCR, summarization, and quiz generation from handwritten notes.


## [Heading 3] Methodology


The project follows an Agile development methodology with iterative prototyping. Development is split into two phases. Phase-1 covers the core platform: user authentication, note upload and organization, community features including voting, comments, and follows, content moderation, and the admin dashboard. Phase-2 focuses on the AI pipeline, covering OCR integration, LLM-powered content expansion, and automated quiz generation. The technology stack uses Next.js 16.1 with React 19.2 for the frontend, leveraging server-side rendering and the App Router pattern. The backend runs on Next.js Server Actions, removing the need for a separate API server. PostgreSQL serves as the database, managed through Drizzle ORM. User authentication uses Better Auth with Google OAuth integration. Image storage is handled through Cloudinary CDN. State management uses Zustand for client state and TanStack Query for server state synchronization.


## [Heading 3] Project Outcome


The expected outcome of this project is a fully functional web platform where students can upload handwritten notes, browse and discover content from other students, and receive AI-enhanced versions of their handwritten material. The platform supports multi-image uploads with proper categorization, a voting system that identifies the most helpful contributions, and real-time notifications for user interactions. The AI pipeline extracts text from handwritten images and produces structured, expanded versions of the content. The platform also includes admin and moderator dashboards for content quality control, a follow system for user engagement, and a responsive design that works across desktop and mobile devices.

## [Heading 3] Introduction


Educational technology has changed how students access academic content over the past decade. Online learning platforms, digital note-sharing tools, and AI-powered study assistants have become common in universities worldwide. However, most of these tools are built for typed or digital-first content. Handwritten notes, which remain a primary study resource for university students in countries like Bangladesh, have received very little attention from educational technology platforms. Students here still rely on physical notebooks and informal messaging groups to share study materials. The gap between available technology and actual student behavior creates an opportunity for a platform that bridges handwritten content with digital tools. OCR technology has reached a point where extracting text from handwriting is practical, and large language models can restructure that extracted text into organized study material. These technologies form the foundation of Prepify's approach.[1].


## [Heading 3] Literature Review


Chatti et al. [1] proposed the Personal Learning Environment (PLE) framework, arguing that learners need personalized spaces to collect, organize, and share educational resources rather than relying on rigid institutional systems. Their framework supports the idea behind Prepify, where students control what they upload and how they organize their study materials. Attwell [2] examined the future of e-learning through personal learning environments and found that students perform better when they have control over their learning resources and can share them within communities. This aligns with Prepify's community-driven approach where voting and commenting let students collectively identify the best content. Getenet et al. [3] studied student attitudes toward digital technology in online learning and found that students engage more with platforms that feel relevant to their existing study habits. Since most Bangladeshi students already share handwritten notes informally, Prepify builds on an existing behavior rather than asking students to adopt something entirely new. Ocenot [4] is a Bangladeshi marketplace for buying and selling academic notes, representing the closest local competitor. Its focus on paid transactions rather than free collaborative sharing highlights the gap Prepify fills. Docsity [5] provides a global library of study notes with ratings and contributor reputation, but its overwhelming volume and premium paywalls limit accessibility for students seeking specific handwritten content. Thinkswap [6] uses a credit-based exchange model that incentivizes uploads but restricts free access, particularly for handwritten problem-solving notes. Google DeepMind [7] introduced the Gemini family of multimodal models capable of processing text, images, and other data types together. These models can understand handwritten content in images and generate structured text from them, which forms the basis of Prepify's AI content enhancement pipeline. Smith [8] provided an overview of the Tesseract OCR engine, which remains one of the most widely used open-source OCR tools. Tesseract's ability to process various scripts and handwriting styles makes it a practical choice for extracting text from student notes. Vaswani et al. [9] introduced the Transformer architecture in their seminal paper, which underpins modern large language models used for text generation, summarization, and content restructuring. This architecture enables the AI features planned for Prepify's Phase-2 pipeline.[2]

## [Heading 4] Similar Applications (Mandatory for project, optional for research)

Docsity is a global platform where students share lecture notes, solved papers, and study guides. It features categorized libraries, ratings, and a reputation system for contributors. However, the platform contains an overwhelming amount of material, and many resources are locked behind premium access, making it difficult for students to quickly find specific handwritten or course-focused notes. Thinkswap allows students to exchange study materials using a credit-based system. It organizes notes by subject and academic level, encouraging contribution through an upload-to-download model. However, its credit and purchase-based access model limits free availability, and handwritten problem-solving notes are often scarce or difficult to navigate. Ocenot is a Bangladeshi marketplace where students buy and sell academic notes, with search options by course and university. It focuses mainly on paid, downloadable materials, and does not offer community-driven ranking or any AI-based features. As a result, it is not optimized for quickly finding free, handwritten problem-solving notes or generating summaries and quizzes from uploaded content. None of these platforms combine free collaborative handwritten note sharing with community ranking and AI-powered content processing, which is the specific gap Prepify addresses.



## [Heading 3] Gap Analysis


The review of existing platforms reveals four key gaps that Prepify addresses. First, no platform offers completely free and open access to handwritten academic notes. Docsity operates on a freemium model where AI features and premium content require payment. Thinkswap uses a credit-based system where students must upload their own materials before downloading others. Ocenot functioned as a paid marketplace where notes were bought and sold. Prepify removes all access barriers entirely, allowing any student to upload, browse, and download handwritten notes without credits, subscriptions, or purchases. Second, no platform is designed specifically around handwritten academic content. Docsity and Thinkswap treat handwritten notes as just another file type alongside typed documents, lecture slides, and textbook solutions. Their search, organization, and AI features are optimized for typed or digital-first content. Prepify is built from the ground up for photographed handwritten solutions, with the entire upload workflow, OCR pipeline, and AI processing tailored for handwriting. Third, there is no platform that serves Bangladeshi university students with localized organization. Docsity and Thinkswap are global platforms where Bangladeshi university courses, departments, and exam patterns are not represented in their category structures. Ocenot was Bangladesh-focused but operated as a commercial marketplace rather than a community platform, and its website is no longer accessible. Prepify organizes content by Bangladeshi universities, departments, and specific courses, making it directly relevant to the local academic context. Fourth, no platform integrates AI processing of handwritten content directly into a free community workflow. While Docsity offers AI summarization and quiz generation through a separate app, these features are behind a paywall and designed for typed documents rather than handwritten notes. Thinkswap and Ocenot offer no AI features at all. Prepify integrates OCR text extraction, AI-powered summarization, and quiz generation directly into the note upload flow at no cost to the user, specifically targeting handwritten academic content.

## [Heading 4] System Design



The system uses a three-tier architecture. ## [Heading 3] Detailed Methodology and Design


Several alternatives were considered during the design phase. For the frontend framework, React with Create React App and Vue.js were evaluated alongside Next.js. Next.js was selected because it provides server-side rendering out of the box, which improves initial load times and SEO, and its App Router with Server Actions eliminates the need for a separate backend server. For image storage, AWS S3 and Firebase Storage were evaluated against Cloudinary. Cloudinary was chosen because it provides automatic image optimization, transformation APIs, and a generous free tier suitable for the project's scale.

## [Heading 3] Environment Setup

The development environment consists of a Windows 11 PC running WebStorm as the primary IDE. Node.js 20 LTS provides the JavaScript runtime environment. The project uses pnpm as the package manager for its strict dependency resolution and disk efficiency. The database runs on Neon's serverless PostgreSQL platform, which provides automatic connection pooling and supports the serverless driver used by the application. Drizzle Kit handles database migrations and schema generation. For local development, the Next.js development server runs with Turbopack for fast hot module replacement. Chrome DevTools and React DevTools are used for debugging. Drizzle Studio provides a visual interface for inspecting and modifying database records during development. Version control uses Git with a GitHub repository. The production deployment runs on a Hostinger VPS with Ubuntu 22.04 LTS, using PM2 as the process manager to keep the Next.js production server running. Cloudinary handles image upload, transformation, and CDN delivery with signed upload presets.


## [Heading 3] Implementation


The implementation of Prepify followed the Agile methodology outlined in Chapter 3, with development organized into iterative sprints. Each sprint delivered a working increment of the platform. The following subsections describe the key modules built during Phase-1.


## [Heading 4] Authentication System


User authentication was implemented using Better Auth with Google OAuth integration. When a user clicks the sign-in button, the application redirects to Google's OAuth consent screen. After the user grants permission, Google returns an authorization code that Better Auth exchanges for user profile data including name, email, and profile image. The session is stored server-side and a session cookie is set in the browser. Session persistence was verified across page reloads and browser restarts. The system also handles edge cases such as revoked permissions and expired tokens.


## [Heading 4] User Onboarding


After first-time authentication, users are directed to an onboarding flow that collects their department, faculty, and batch information. This data is stored in the user profile and used to personalize the content feed and organize notes by academic context. The onboarding form uses cascading dropdowns where faculty selection filters the available departments.


## [Heading 4] Notes Upload System


The note upload module supports multi-file image uploads through Cloudinary's signed upload presets. Users fill a form with a title, description, and course selection, then attach one or more images of their handwritten notes in JPEG, PNG, or WebP format up to 10MB per file. Each image is uploaded to Cloudinary with automatic optimization and transformation applied. The upload component provides progress tracking and error handling for failed uploads. Resource links can also be attached to provide additional references alongside the handwritten content.


## [Heading 4] Voting System


The voting module implements a like and unlike mechanism with optimistic UI updates. When a user clicks the like button, the vote count updates immediately on the client side through Zustand state management, while the server action processes the database write asynchronously. A unique constraint on the user-note combination in the database prevents duplicate votes. Each like adds one point to the note author's leaderboard score through a transactional database operation that updates both the likes table and the leaderboard points atomically.


## [Heading 4] Leaderboard


The leaderboard ranks users by their total contribution points, which are calculated from the likes received on their approved notes. The points system uses a transaction log that records each point addition and subtraction for auditability. The leaderboard page displays the top contributors with their profile information, total points, and number of approved notes.


## [Heading 4] Content Moderation System


Every uploaded note enters a moderation queue with a pending status. Administrators and moderators review submissions through a dedicated dashboard that shows pending notes with their images, metadata, and uploader information. Moderators can approve notes to make them publicly visible or reject them with a reason that is communicated to the author. The note status workflow follows a pending to approved or rejected pipeline, with status badges displayed on the author's My Notes page to provide clear feedback on each submission's state.


## [Heading 4] Comment System


The commenting module supports threaded discussions under each note. Users can create, edit, and delete their own comments. Comment creation triggers a notification to the note author. The system uses server actions for all comment operations with proper authorization checks to ensure users can only modify their own comments, while moderators and admins can remove any comment.


## [Heading 4] Follow System and Notifications


Users can follow other contributors to track their activity. When a user follows another, the followed user receives an email notification through the Brevo API. The follow relationship is stored in a many-to-many table with unique constraints preventing duplicate follows. The notification system also sends emails when a followed user uploads new content and when notes receive approval or rejection status changes.


## [Heading 4] Home Page and Navigation


The landing page features a hero section, a display of recently approved notes, a how-it-works guide, platform statistics showing total users and notes, and a department browser for filtering content by academic department. The navigation system uses Next.js App Router with server-side rendering for initial page loads and client-side transitions for subsequent navigation.


## [Heading 4] Admin and Moderator Dashboards


The admin dashboard provides platform-wide statistics including total registered users, total uploaded notes, and the count of pending moderation items. Admins can manage user roles, promote users to moderator status, and view all platform activity. The moderator dashboard provides a focused notes review queue where moderators can efficiently process pending submissions with approve and reject actions.


## [Heading 4] Responsive Design and Dark Mode


The user interface adapts to screen widths from 375px mobile to 1920px desktop without layout breaks. The responsive design uses CSS media queries and flexible grid layouts to adjust component sizing and arrangement at each breakpoint. Dark mode support is implemented through next-themes with automatic system preference detection, allowing users to switch between light and dark color schemes or follow their operating system setting.


## [Heading 3] Testing and Evaluation


Testing followed a manual approach with structured test cases covering all major user flows. The authentication flow was tested by logging in with multiple Google accounts and verifying session persistence across page reloads. Note upload was tested with single and multiple image files in JPEG, PNG, and WebP formats, confirming that Cloudinary correctly processes and stores each file with appropriate transformations. The voting system was verified by checking that upvotes correctly increment the note's like count and the author's leaderboard points, and that a user cannot vote on the same note twice, enforced by the database unique constraint. The moderation workflow was tested by uploading notes with a regular account, then logging into admin and moderator accounts to approve and reject submissions, confirming that status changes propagate correctly and that rejected notes show the rejection reason to the author. The follow and notification system was tested by having one account follow another, then verifying that the followed user receives an email notification through Brevo. Comment creation, editing, and deletion were tested across different user roles. Responsive design was verified across Chrome, Firefox, and Safari on desktop, and on Android devices at various screen widths. Page load performance was measured using Chrome Lighthouse, with the landing page achieving scores above 85 for performance and above 90 for accessibility. Page load performance was measured using Chrome Lighthouse on the production deployment. The landing page achieved a performance score of 87 and an accessibility score of 92. The note detail page scored 85 for performance with image-heavy content loading through Cloudinary's CDN with automatic WebP conversion and lazy loading. Server-side rendered pages deliver initial HTML within 800 milliseconds on average, with full interactivity achieved within 1.5 seconds on standard broadband connections. The Cloudinary CDN reduces image load times by serving optimized formats from edge locations closest to the user. Database query performance was monitored through Neon's dashboard, with the most complex queries such as leaderboard aggregation with joins across users, notes, and likes tables completing within 120 milliseconds.

## [Heading 1] Engineering Standards and Design Challenges


This chapter discusses the engineering standards applied in Prepify, the impact of the project on society and the environment, the financial analysis, and the mapping to complex engineering problem and activity categories.


## [Heading 3] Compliance with the Standards


Prepify follows several industry standards across software development, communication, and security. Each standard was selected after evaluating alternatives based on project requirements, cost, and compatibility.


## [Heading 4] Software Standards: The platform follows the ECMAScript 2024 (ES15) standard for JavaScript, ensuring compatibility with modern browsers and runtime environments. TypeScript 5.0 strict mode is used for static type checking, which catches type errors at compile time rather than runtime. The coding style follows the ESLint recommended configuration with Next.js-specific rules. For the database, PostgreSQL follows the SQL:2023 standard with ACID compliance for transaction integrity. The REST architectural style guides API endpoint design, though Server Actions handle most data operations. Alternative standards considered included CommonJS modules (rejected in favor of ES modules for tree-shaking support) and JavaScript without TypeScript (rejected because the complex relational schema benefits from compile-time type safety).


## [Heading 4] Hardware Standards: The application is deployed on a Hostinger VPS running Ubuntu 22.04 LTS with 2 vCPUs, 8GB RAM, and 100GB NVMe SSD storage. The server meets the minimum hardware requirements for running Node.js 20 LTS and serving the Next.js application. Development was done on a PC with 16GB RAM and an Intel processor. Mobile testing was performed on Android devices with screen sizes ranging from 5.5 to 6.7 inches to verify responsive design breakpoints. An alternative deployment option considered was Vercel's serverless platform, which would eliminate hardware management entirely. The VPS was chosen because it provides more control over the deployment environment and costs less at the current usage level.


## [Heading 4] Communication Standards: All client-server communication uses HTTPS (TLS 1.3) for encrypted data transmission. The platform uses HTTP/2 for multiplexed connections, reducing latency for concurrent resource loading. OAuth 2.0 with PKCE (Proof Key for Code Exchange) handles the Google authentication flow. Email notifications use the SMTP protocol through the Brevo API. Image uploads to Cloudinary use their REST API over HTTPS with signed upload presets for security. An alternative considered was WebSocket for real-time updates, but HTTP polling with TanStack Query's refetch intervals was sufficient for the current feature set and simpler to implement.



## [Heading 3] Impact on Society, Environment and Sustainability


## [Heading 4] Impact on Life: Prepify directly benefits university students by reducing the time they spend searching for study materials before exams. Instead of scrolling through chat groups or asking contacts one by one, students can browse organized notes filtered by course and department. The voting system surfaces the most helpful content, so students spend less time evaluating whether a particular set of notes is worth studying. For students who contribute notes, the leaderboard and points system provides recognition for their effort, which may encourage more students to share their work.


## [Heading 4] Impact on Society and Environment: The platform promotes collaborative learning within university communities. By making quality study materials accessible to all students, not just those with the right social connections, it reduces information inequality. The environmental impact is minimal since the platform is entirely digital and does not require physical resources beyond the server hardware. Cloud hosting through shared infrastructure is more energy-efficient than individual local servers. The platform may reduce paper usage if students begin storing and accessing notes digitally rather than photocopying handwritten pages.


## [Heading 4] Ethical Aspects: The platform collects only the minimum personal data required for authentication: name, email, and profile image from Google OAuth. No passwords are stored locally. The content moderation system ensures that inappropriate material is reviewed before becoming publicly visible. Users retain ownership of their uploaded content. The platform does not sell user data to third parties. AI features in Phase-2 process note content through API calls but do not store or train models on user data.


## [Heading 4] Sustainability Plan: The platform is designed to be financially sustainable at low cost. Current operational expenses total approximately $8 per month ($6.99 for VPS hosting and $5/year for the domain). Both Neon PostgreSQL and Cloudinary operate on free tiers that support the current user base. If the platform grows, a freemium model with premium features such as unlimited AI processing credits or ad-free experience could generate revenue. The codebase is open-source and well-documented, allowing future developers or university IT departments to maintain and extend it.



## [Heading 3] Project Management and Financial Analysis


The project budget for Prepify is approximately 11,000 BDT. The breakdown is as follows: Neon PostgreSQL runs on the free tier with 0.5GB storage, of which approximately 100MB is currently used. Cloudinary operates on its free tier with 25GB storage, of which approximately 1GB is used for note images. The VPS hosting on Hostinger with 2 vCPU cores and 8GB RAM costs 10,000 BDT. The Brevo email service runs on the free tier supporting 300 emails per day, with current usage at approximately 10 emails per day. The domain name (prepify.space) costs 1,000 BDT for one year of registration. The total project cost comes to 11,000 BDT. An alternate budget using premium cloud services was considered: AWS EC2 t3.medium (approximately 3,500 BDT/month), AWS RDS PostgreSQL (approximately 1,750 BDT/month), and AWS S3 with CloudFront (approximately 600 BDT/month), which would total roughly 70,000 BDT annually. The current budget was chosen because it provides adequate performance for the expected user base while keeping costs within a student developer's budget. The platform does not currently generate revenue, but potential revenue models include a freemium tier for unlimited AI processing credits, institutional licensing for universities, and non-intrusive advertising.

## [Heading 1] Conclusion


This chapter summarizes the work completed, discusses the limitations of the current implementation, and outlines directions for future development.



## [Heading 3] Summary

Prepify was developed as a Final Year Design Project to address the problem of disorganized handwritten note sharing among university students in Bangladesh. The project delivered a fully functional web platform built with Next.js 16.1, React 19.2, PostgreSQL, and Cloudinary. Phase-1 implementation covered all core features: user authentication through Google OAuth, multi-image note uploads with course-based categorization, a community voting system with a points-based leaderboard, commenting and follow features with email notifications, and a content moderation workflow with admin and moderator dashboards. The platform uses server-side rendering for fast page loads and responsive design that works on both desktop and mobile devices. The Agile development approach with iterative prototyping allowed continuous refinement of features based on testing feedback. All five project objectives defined in Chapter 1 have been addressed in the current implementation, with the AI-powered content enhancement pipeline prepared architecturally for Phase-2 completion.

## [Heading 3] Limitation

The current implementation has several limitations. First, the AI pipeline for OCR and content enhancement is not yet operational in production. While the architecture supports it, the actual integration with Tesseract.js and Gemini API is part of Phase-2 work. Second, the OCR accuracy for Bengali handwriting is expected to be lower than for English text, and no fallback mechanism has been implemented yet. Third, the platform currently supports only image uploads; PDF document processing is planned but not yet built. Fourth, the leaderboard algorithm uses a simple points-based calculation that does not account for the quality of votes received or the age of contributions. Fifth, the platform has been tested with a small number of users and has not undergone load testing with hundreds of concurrent users. Sixth, the notification system uses email only; in-app push notifications are not yet supported.

## [Heading 3] Future Work

Several directions for future work have been identified. The immediate priority is completing the AI pipeline in Phase-2, which includes integrating OCR for handwritten text extraction, connecting the Gemini API for content expansion and restructuring, and building an automated quiz generation feature from uploaded notes. Beyond Phase-2, planned improvements include support for PDF and document uploads, in-app push notifications using web push APIs, a mobile application built with React Native for offline access, Redis caching for improved performance at scale, multi-university support with institution verification, collaborative study groups where multiple students can annotate shared notes, and an advanced recommendation engine that suggests relevant notes based on a student's department, courses, and browsing history.
